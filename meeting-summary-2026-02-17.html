Strategic Analysis

This day was designed to align. It partially succeeded at informing. It mostly failed at listening. What follows is an honest reading of how the day flowed, where it broke down, and where the highest-leverage interventions actually sit — using Donella Meadows' framework for thinking about systems, not just symptoms.

What Frustrated Me

  • I couldn't get airtime in the session that mattered most. The AV breakout was the one block where my voice should have been centered. Instead, the conversation moved at Dom's pace — which is fast, technical, and dominated by whoever has the most detail to share in the moment. I had to interject to contribute. The post-it system (1 per person) further compressed what could be expressed. Items I cared about didn't make the board because the conversation had already moved.
  • Rare in-person time was spent being talked at. We're together maybe once or twice a year with the people who can actually change things. Roughly 80% of the day was presentations. Financial reviews, scorecard explanations, sales strategy — all information that could have been a pre-read or a recorded video. The opportunity cost of using this time for one-directional information transfer is enormous and invisible in the agenda.
  • "This is gonna all start with us." Every operational problem — flow sheets, planning tools, content management, training sourcing — was pushed to the property team to solve. The stated reason sounds reasonable ("we don't know what works for you"). The effect is that the team builds solutions that may be adopted, replaced, or ignored when corporate eventually delivers a platform. It's empowerment without resources. Eric asked the hardest question of the day — "when does it start going from the top down?" — and the answer was: not yet.
  • The feedback collection has no visible accountability mechanism. Whiteboards were photographed. Post-its were placed. Verbal commitments were made. But nothing was assigned a timeline, an owner, or a check-in date. The gap between collecting feedback and acting on it is where trust erodes. If the ETC training doesn't happen, if the switcher doesn't get replaced, if the renovation scoping process doesn't change — then next year's offsite will have the same post-its on the same board.
  • The Courtney moment. The SVP of Operations walked in, delivered cookies, said "I'm gonna leave you to do things I don't understand," and left. The person accountable for operations at the executive level has no technical understanding of the AV function. When budget conversations happen, when renovation scoping happens, when tool decisions get made — there's no advocate at the leadership table who understands what this team does or needs.
  • The Decision Tree asks individuals to solve structural problems. The workshop framed decision bottlenecks as confidence issues: you know the answer, just trust yourself. But the AV breakout revealed that many of our bottlenecks are organizational: no budget at 311, no planning system replacement, no CATs from architects, no renovation scoping involvement. You can't confidence your way past a missing budget line. The framework is useful for day-of event calls. It's useless for the problems that actually slow this team down.

Highest Leverage Interventions

Donella Meadows identified twelve places to intervene in a system, ranked from least to most effective. Tweaking parameters (budgets, headcounts) is the weakest leverage. Changing information flows, rules, and system goals is where real change lives. Here are the leverage points that matter most for what I saw today — starting with the highest leverage and working down.

#6
Information Flows
Meadows: "The structure of who does and does not have access to information"

What's broken

The information in this organization flows up and out, not down and across. Leadership presents financials and strategies to the field. The field reports problems through tickets that compete with every other property globally. But the field's operational knowledge — what actually works in these rooms, what clients actually need, what breaks under load — doesn't flow to the people who make structural decisions (renovation scoping, tool selection, budget allocation). Pollard visited Chicago for the first time last month. The SVP of Operations self-identified as not understanding AV work. The scorecard was explained for the first time to a team that's been measured by it.

Why this is the highest leverage here

Almost every problem surfaced today is an information flow problem. The lighting glitch persists because the ticket trail is fragmented. The V-600 was spec'd wrong because on-site input wasn't part of the renovation scoping. The Decision Tree is needed because people don't know what they're authorized to decide. The sales strategy required translation because it wasn't designed for this audience. Fix the information flows and you fix half the symptoms without touching anything else.

What I can do from here Write things down. This document is an information flow intervention. The ZV feedback doc Justin and I compiled is an information flow intervention. Every ticket with fiscal impact noted is an information flow intervention. The team that documents best gets heard first — not because the system is fair, but because documented problems are the only ones that survive the distance between the field and the people who allocate resources.
#5
Rules of the System
Meadows: "Incentives, punishments, constraints — who has power over them"

What's broken

The scorecard weights Finance at 40% and Engagement at 15%. That's a rule. It tells every property: cost control is your primary mission. It doesn't matter how many presentations emphasize engagement — the measurement system says otherwise, and people optimize for what's measured. Similarly, the rule "start solutions at the property level" sounds like autonomy but functions as a constraint: it means the organization never has to build shared infrastructure until someone below builds a prototype worth stealing.

Why this matters

The Decision Tree workshop taught people to categorize their decisions. But the real decision rules — who approves what, what budgets exist, who gets consulted during renovations — were not on the table. Those rules determine whether your trunk decision can ever become a branch. No amount of empowerment workshops change the rules. Only changing the rules changes the rules.

What I can do from here I can't change the scorecard weights. But I can make the rules visible. When I document why a decision required trunk-level approval — when a ticket shows the fiscal cost of waiting for validation that could have been pre-authorized — that makes the implicit rules explicit. Explicit rules can be questioned. Invisible ones can't. Eric's question ("when does it start going from the top down?") was the most important sentence spoken all day because it named a rule that everyone else was just living inside of.
#4
Power to Self-Organize
Meadows: "The power to add, change, or evolve system structure"

What's broken

The AV team has the technical knowledge to design its own operational infrastructure: flow sheets, content management processes, event planning workflows, training curricula. And in fact, the team is already doing this — Google Sheets for flow sheets, DIY solutions for content management, self-sourced training. But the team doesn't have the authority or resources to make these solutions durable. They're building with duct tape because the organization hasn't provided lumber.

Why this matters

The healthiest version of "start from the property level" would be: the team builds solutions, leadership provides resources to make them permanent, and the best solutions become organizational standards. Right now it's: the team builds solutions, leadership promises a platform (Lasso, ClickUp replacement), the platform either arrives late or doesn't fit, and the team rebuilds. Self-organization without institutional support is just extra work.

What I can do from here Build things worth adopting. The flow sheet I helped develop, the ZV feedback doc, the approach to content management — if these are clean, documented, and shareable, they become harder to ignore or replace with something worse. The team that builds the best prototype doesn't just solve its own problem; it sets the default for everyone else. That's self-organization becoming system structure.
#9
Delays
Meadows: "The lengths of time relative to the rates of system change"

What's broken

The delay between problem identification and resolution is the dominant friction in this system. The lighting relay glitch has been ticketed multiple times — the person assigned to fix it quit, and the issue persists. ClickUp was removed months ago with no replacement. The Lasso planning system is "4-6 weeks" away — the same distance it's probably been for a while. The AVOS budget at 311 has never existed. These delays aren't bugs; they're the system working as designed for a company growing faster than its operational infrastructure can keep up.

Why this matters

Every delay has a cost, but the costs are borne by the field team — not by the people who control the delay. When a ticket sits unresolved, the on-site team builds a workaround. When a tool is removed without replacement, the team invents a process. The delay is invisible to leadership because the symptoms are absorbed before they reach the dashboard. This is why documenting fiscal impact on tickets matters: it makes the cost of delay visible to the people who can shorten it.

What I can do from here Shorten the delays I can control. Submit tickets immediately, not after collecting months of evidence. Follow up on the Pollard visit scheduling this week while the momentum is fresh. Forward the ZV doc to helpdesk before the end of this week. And for the delays I can't control — the Lasso timeline, the 311 budget, the renovation scoping process — track them visibly so the delay itself becomes documented evidence.
#8
Balancing Feedback Loops
Meadows: "The strength of the feedbacks relative to the impacts they are trying to correct"

What's broken

The offsite is supposed to be a feedback loop: leadership visits, hears from the field, adjusts course. But the loop is too slow (annual or semi-annual), too compressed (one day for everything), and too asymmetric (80% presenting, 20% listening). The engagement surveys are another feedback loop — now reduced from 4x to 2x per year, making them slower. The ticket system is a third loop, but it measures resolution of individual issues, not patterns across issues.

Why this matters

A feedback loop that's too slow or too weak relative to the system it's trying to correct is functionally decorative. The annual offsite collects a year's worth of frustration and compresses it into post-it notes. By the time leadership acts — if they act — the field has already built workarounds, and the original problem has mutated. The AV breakout was the strongest feedback loop of the day, precisely because Dom has the technical authority to act on what he hears in real time (the switcher budget redirect). The other sessions had no equivalent authority in the room.

What I can do from here Create shorter, more frequent feedback loops within my sphere. The Pollard room-by-room walkthrough in March is a feedback loop. Regular ticket submissions with impact notes is a feedback loop. Peer QAA walkthroughs between properties is a feedback loop. None of these require organizational permission. They just require consistency.
The meta-observation
The highest-leverage intervention available to an IC is always information flow. I can't change the scorecard weights, the reporting structure, or the renovation scoping process. But I can make problems legible, make costs visible, and make solutions shareable. That's not a consolation prize — it's the mechanism by which field knowledge eventually becomes organizational knowledge. The question is whether this organization's feedback loops are fast enough to convert field knowledge into structural change before the next offsite covers the same ground.

Decision Tree Workshop

Company-wide framework for categorizing decisions by risk level and required approval. Presented as a tool to "empower" faster decision-making across all departments.

Leaves
You decide. You act. No reporting needed. Low risk, automatic, part of daily flow.
AV example: adjusting LED brightness, basic room setup, standard equipment checks.
Branches
You decide. You act. Then you inform someone. Low-to-medium risk, may have downstream business impacts.
AV example: adding uplights to a reception after a session issue, tweaking a room setup that doesn't match the diagram, swapping a failing cable/device during an event.
Trunk
You decide. You get approval. Then you act. Medium risk, medium lift. You have the solution but need a green light.
AV example: comping microphones for a pressured client, recommending a specific equipment rental for a complex event, adjusting staffing for overhire coverage.
Roots
High-impact decisions made jointly. High risk, hard to reverse, requires full buy-in from leadership.
AV example: proposing a $9K equipment purchase instead of continued rentals, changing a vendor relationship, major workflow changes.

How It Played Out in the Room

Departments broke into groups and categorized their post-it decisions onto hand-drawn trees. Key observations from the share-out:

  • AV team: Had no difficulty categorizing. The challenge wasn't knowing which level a decision belongs to — it was that some decisions shift levels depending on who's on site (tech manager vs. AVOS) and how the escalation chain works for overhire coverage.
  • Service team: Because Willis has two service managers, many "leaf" decisions become "branches" by default — they need to check with each other. Shared responsibility structurally inflates the decision level.
  • Culinary: Chefs categorized easily. Trunk and root decisions came naturally because their work is inherently higher-stakes (ingredient changes affect dish integrity, client-facing menus).
  • EPMs: Struggled with root-level decisions because they're long-term and compete for attention against the flood of daily leaf/branch work.

The Trunk Bottleneck

Facilitator identified the trunk as the primary bottleneck: "You've already made a decision, but you have to get approval before you can execute." The stated goal is to move trunk decisions up to branches over time — empowering people to act and report rather than wait for a green light.

What the framework misses

The Decision Tree diagnoses trunk delays as a confidence problem: people know the right answer but feel they need validation. The AV breakout told a different story. Many of our bottlenecks aren't confidence issues — they're structural: no budget at 311, no planning system, no CATs from architects, no renovation scoping involvement. No amount of individual empowerment fixes a missing budget line. The framework is useful for day-of event decisions but doesn't address the organizational infrastructure gaps that actually slow us down.

One genuinely useful example: the EPM team shared how they moved deadline management from trunk to branch by building in client-facing buffer deadlines. The real deadline is internal; the client deadline has padding. This eliminated the need to check with chefs/service/GM every time a client missed a deadline. A process change that enabled a decision-level change.

What Each Category Contains

Finance & Efficiency — 40%

Four sub-categories: Cost of Goods Sold (food, nourish, alcohol, business services), Labor (full-time + part-time + benefits + insurance), Controllables (operating supplies, cleaning, repairs, contract services, utilities, plant maintenance), and EBIDAR (earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and rent). They use EBIDAR rather than EBITDA because the operations team has zero control over rent — that's a real estate negotiation.

Why 40%: "This is the one you have the most control over." Managing to budget, protecting the bottom line so the company can reinvest in properties and people.

Revenue & Growth — 15%

Three sub-categories at 5% each: Top Line, AV Revenue, Post-Contract Growth. Revenue is weighted low because "a lot of revenue is controlled by the commercial team, not the operations team." But operations enables it — client experience drives retention and upsell.

Post-contract growth at 311: $600K (11% of total revenue). At Willis: $2.7M (18% of total revenue). These are meaningful numbers driven primarily by on-site upselling.

Customer NPS — 15%

Single metric: Net Promoter Score. Calculated as % promoters (9-10) minus % detractors (0-6). Passives (7-8) dilute the score without contributing. Average hospitality NPS is in the 50s-60s. Convene targets 95+ and is tracking at or near 100 at both Chicago properties.

Key nuance: Two 8s at the start of the year tanked 2025 scores because the survey volume was low. It took months to climb back from 77. Volume of responses matters as much as scores. The team needs more surveys returned and needs every interaction to push for 9-10, not 7-8.

Employee Engagement — 15%

Based on survey scores. Moving to 2x/year surveys in 2026 (down from 4). 130+ internal promotions company-wide in 2025 (operations side only). The stated priority: transparent one-on-ones, development conversations, recognition through proper channels.

QAA (Compliance) — 15%

Physical and procedural standards. Maintenance of spaces, assets, products. The framing: "if employee engagement is the soul, QAA is the body." 311 missed its QAA goal in 2025 (86.3). The plan: peer QAA walkthroughs between properties, fresh-eyes audits, whole-team accountability (not just managers), daily or at least weekly walkthroughs.

The weighting tells a story
Finance at 40% and engagement at 15% is a 2.67:1 ratio. The organization measures what the team costs nearly three times as heavily as how the team feels. A significant portion of the day's presentations emphasized how much engagement matters — but the scorecard, the actual measurement tool, doesn't match the rhetoric. This isn't a criticism of the people presenting; it's a structural observation about what the organization optimizes for.

Organizational Observations

Centralized engineering, decentralized operations, no bridge

Pollard's AV Engineering team is remote (UK-based, with Craig as a new addition). Pollard visited Chicago for the first time just last month. The ticket system is the official interface between on-site teams and engineering, but the on-site team's lived experience is that tickets go into a queue that competes with every other property globally. Meanwhile, operational tools (flow sheets, planning, content management) are built at the property level because nothing comes from above. The org chart says these teams are connected. The day's conversations suggest they're connected by a ticket queue and occasional Slack messages.

Tool removal without replacement is a pattern

ClickUp was removed. No replacement. Lasso is "4-6 weeks" away. Content management has no solution. Rental laptop email is being figured out. Property-level flow sheets are DIY. The AVOS budget at 311 was never created. These aren't individual oversights — this is an organization that announces changes faster than it implements them, and the on-site teams absorb the gap by building their own solutions.

"Start from the property level" protects leadership from accountability

Every operational problem discussed in the AV breakout — flow sheets, planning, content management, training sourcing — was pushed to the property team to solve first. The stated reason: "the things I think might make sense might make no sense to anybody else." The effect: the team builds solutions that may or may not be adopted, replaced, or ignored when corporate eventually delivers a platform. Eric's question — "when does it start going from the top down?" — was the most direct challenge of the day. The answer was essentially: not yet.

The feedback loop is long and opaque

The day collected a lot of information: whiteboard items, post-it votes, decision tree exercises, verbal feedback. Photos were taken. But there's no visible mechanism for turning this into accountable action. No timeline was committed to. No one said "the switcher replacement will happen by X date" or "ETC training will be scheduled in Q2." The gap between collecting feedback and acting on it is where trust erodes.

The day was designed for leadership to present, not to listen

Of roughly 8 hours, approximately 6 were presentations (financial review, food/bev, decision tree lecture, scorecard, sales strategy). The AV breakout and the decision tree activity were the only blocks where the team's voice was centered. For a gathering that happens once or twice a year, and for a team that's rarely in the same room with corporate leadership, the ratio says: we have things to tell you > we need to learn from you.

My Experience of the Day

The AV breakout conversation moved at Dom's pace — fast, technical, tangent-prone. Dom is knowledgeable and clearly cares, but his facilitation style favors people who interject. I had things to contribute that didn't make it onto the board because the conversation moved before I could find an opening.
